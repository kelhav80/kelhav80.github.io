A Bronx Tale

Throughout my childhood, it was hard for me to understand whenever someone talked about New York City, they never mentioned the Bronx, my home. How often would you see the Bronx on a “Best New York tour guide”? I don’t think I ever saw that. “Which borough has the best food?”, “Which borough has the best spots?”, etc. You may say I’m biased but I will always say the Bronx. Unfortunately, it never fails to be the first borough named when it comes to all the negativity about New York City. “Which borough is the most dangerous?”, “Am I going to get robbed there?”, “Which borough has the worst personalities?” Ask anyone these questions and they probably say the Bronx. But, my whole life I never felt this way; I could walk anywhere and not feel in danger. People would say you’re just not a prime target or you just have terrible situational awareness. Those same people would never take the red or green line uptown to see for themselves.

No one seems to understand the Bronx, this isn’t something very recent either. It's been like this before I was born, before my parents got their first apartment on Boston Road, before they landed in America. “The Bronx is Burning” was an era when many neighborhoods and buildings in the Bronx were burned down. Almost 300,000 people lost their homes and were displaced. The Bronx was labeled as a place of poverty and a home for druggies, completely ignoring the cultural renaissance and rise in Hip-Hop that occurred within the community. People would make movies, TV shows, music, and share stories of their experiences. Talking about the hardship and what it was like to live in this borough. To some it was eye-opening; to others however, it was confirmation bias that this borough was dirty and poverty ridden with dangerous people walking around. Never bothered to go up here unless they wanted to watch a Yankees game or see some animals. Fearing the people who walk by and clutching their bags. That stigma would stick for years until my parents landed in America, until they got their first apartment on Boston Road, until I was born, and even now.

No place is perfect; no person is perfect in the slightest. But the way the people of the Bronx are viewed is constantly in a negative light. The people I see on the streets, buses, and trains everyday are not dangerous. No matter how loud they are on the phone or how scary you think they dress. Don’t make assumptions when some people here have so much pride for this borough. We know it's not perfect, we know the apartments aren’t as good as boroughs or maybe the attractions aren’t as appealing as the other boroughs. We don’t care at all; we do way more explaining to justify than anyone else. So many leaders have risen up in this community and bring down these negative stereotypes that plague our home. We don’t have to change the way we walk, talk, dress, or speak. Bronx residents have more pride than anyone else because we go beyond to prove ourselves. We have been trying to improve ourselves since the 90s, before I was born, before my parents got their first apartment on Boston Road, before they landed in America. Our community is so knitted together and always brings each other up even when not everyone can make it. Ever since I was a kid, I’ve gone to my cousin's house on Sedgwick Ave, conveniently right in front of RPT: River Park Towers. These thirty-eight story towers house over five thousand tenants and are notorious for being dangerous and a breeding ground for the most hood and gangster people. I know multiple people who live there, all A+ students. They aren’t some thugs and gangsters because of the way they talk. They aren’t thugs because of the way they dress and speak. And they for sure aren’t thugs, gangsters, hoodrats, hoodlums, or criminals because of the place they so happen to live in. You can talk to them and see who they are; participating in community-building events all the time. I go to every single one of them and I see a community, had everyone from another borough gone they might have thought it was a gang meetup but I saw a community coming together, helping each other. But the news will never talk about that. They’ll talk about the story of someone being hit by a stray bullet for however long they can before they get a new story to shove down your throat. They have been doing this since my parents landed in America, since they got their first apartment on Boston Road, since I was born, and they will continue to do it.

You can’t be scared of this borough if you can’t get off on 174th Street and ask the people smoking under the train stairs for directions. You can’t be scared of the Bronx if you go to a Yankees game and run to the 4 Train to get out there as soon as possible, instead of staying for a while and appreciating the place you took the time to go to. You can’t be scared of the Bronx if all you have information about this borough is the violent stories you read on Instagram or TikTok. You can’t be scared of the Bronx if you think everyone is a gangbanger with no future ahead of them. You can’t be scared of the Bronx if you think the dream of every kid here is to be the next big drill rapper and the only skill they have is to get sturdy. The Bronx isn’t scary. I’ve never been scared to walk home. Don’t be scared.
